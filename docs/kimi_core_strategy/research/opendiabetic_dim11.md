# Dimension 11: Diabetes Cognitive Load, Burnout & Lifestyle Support Gaps

## Executive Summary

This research dimension examines the daily burden of diabetes management, encompassing cognitive load, burnout, and lifestyle support needs for people living with diabetes (PwD). The findings reveal a profound and multifaceted burden that extends far beyond blood glucose monitoring. Key findings include: **79% of PwD experience diabetes burnout**[^1^], with **75% of burned-out patients discontinuing treatment**[^1^]. People with diabetes make **over 180 health-related decisions daily**[^2^], and the total estimated time for recommended self-care is **~4 hours per day for adults and >5 hours for children**[^3^]. Alarm fatigue from CGMs affects **25-50% of users**, leading to device discontinuation[^4^]. Stigma affects **more than half of PwD**[^5^], and **24.3% experience moderate diabetes distress with 6.6% experiencing severe distress**[^6^]. The evidence overwhelmingly demonstrates that patients are not seeking more complexity—they are seeking simplification, autonomy support, and tools that reduce rather than add to their daily burden.

## 1. Diabetes Burnout: Prevalence, Causes, and Interventions

### 1.1 The Scale of the Burnout Crisis

Diabetes burnout represents one of the most significant psychosocial complications of diabetes. The IDF's global survey across seven countries found that **79% of participants report experiencing diabetes burnout**, primarily due to the emotional strain and demands of daily management[^1^]. Alarmingly, **3 in 4 of those affected by burnout admitted to stopping or interrupting their diabetes treatment** due to stress or feeling overwhelmed[^1^].

In the United States, the CDC's 2021 National Health Interview Survey data revealed that **24.3% of US adults with diabetes had moderate diabetes distress** (95% CI: 22.5%–26.1%) and **6.6% had severe diabetes distress** (95% CI: 5.6%–7.8%)[^6^]. The prevalence was notably higher among people aged 18 to 64 years, women, and those with lower incomes[^6^].

### 1.2 The Three Dimensions of Diabetes Burnout

Recent research has identified three interdependent dimensions of diabetes burnout: **exhaustion, detachment, and loss of control**[^8^]. These dimensions interact dynamically, resulting in various burnout profiles that individuals may experience throughout their journey with the condition[^8^]. A modified Delphi study for developing a Diabetes Burnout Scale for T2D identified consensus domains including: psychological stress, emotional exhaustion, physical fatigue, and disengagement from diabetes management[^8^].

Context: Modified Delphi study for developing a Diabetes Burnout Scale for T2D
Confidence: High


### 1.3 Subdimensions of Diabetes-Related Distress

A large French cohort study of 1,220 adults with type 1 diabetes used the Problem Areas in Diabetes (PAID) questionnaire and identified six data-driven subdimensions of diabetes distress: **emotional distress, fear of complications, social distress, eating distress, management distress, and diabetes burnout**[^9^]. The leading subdimensions were fear of complications (mean score 50.1/100) and diabetes burnout (mean score 45.9/100)[^9^]. Notably, **48.5% of participants reported high diabetes distress (PAID ≥40)**, and similar DD levels were observed across HbA1c levels and treatment modalities, including automated insulin delivery and CGM use[^9^].


Claim: 48.5% of adults with T1D reported high diabetes distress; fear of complications and diabetes burnout were leading subdimensions

Date: 2025-02-24
Excerpt: "In total, 1220 participants... had a total mean PAID score of 39.6 (21.7) and 592 (48.5%) reported high DD. Leading subdimensions of DD included fear of complications (50.1 (24.4)) and diabetes burnout (45.9 (24.5))."
Context: Large diverse French cohort of adults with type 1 diabetes
Confidence: High


### 1.4 Effective Interventions for Diabetes Distress

A systematic review and meta-analysis of 18 studies (1,639 participants) found that **psychological interventions significantly reduced diabetes distress in the short-term** (SMD= -0.56; 95% CI= -0.90, -0.22; p = 0.001)[^10^]. Interventions types included mindfulness-based therapy, cognitive behavioral therapy, motivational interviewing, problem-solving therapy, and acceptance and commitment therapy. Subgroup analysis indicated that effects were enhanced when delivered in **group format**, by **psychologists**, using a **technology component**, or including participants with **elevated baseline distress**[^10^].


Claim: Psychological interventions significantly reduced diabetes distress (SMD=-0.56) in the short-term; effects enhanced with group format and technology
Source: BMC Psychiatry (Zu et al.)
URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12888-024-06125-z
Date: 2024-10-08
Excerpt: "We found that psychological interventions that measured diabetes distress significantly reduced diabetes distress in the short-term in people with type 2 diabetes (SMD= -0.56; 95% CI= -0.90, -0.22; p = 0.001)... delivered in a group format (SMD= -0.65), by psychologist (SMD= -0.93), using a technology component."
Context: Meta-analysis of 18 studies, 1,639 participants with type 2 diabetes
Confidence: High


However, the meta-analysis found that short- and long-term effects on HbA1c were non-significant, and the long-term effect on diabetes distress was also non-significant (SMD = -0.45; p=0.07)[^10^], suggesting that sustained intervention may be needed for lasting benefits.

## 2. The Daily Cognitive Load of Diabetes: Decision Fatigue and Mental Burden

### 2.1 The 180+ Daily Decisions

Research estimates that people with diabetes make **more than 180 health-related decisions every single day**[^2^]. A Stanford University study found that people with T1D make around **120 or more additional decisions each day** compared to people without diabetes[^11^]. These decisions span multiple categories:

- **Insulin dosing decisions**: calculating units based on current blood sugar, planned carbohydrate intake, and anticipated activity
- **Food calculations**: estimating carbs in meals, considering how different foods affect blood sugar
- **Activity adjustments**: determining if blood sugar is safe for exercise, planning for potential drops
- **Correction protocols**: interpreting blood sugar readings, deciding when to intervene
- **Preventive measures**: timing checks, planning ahead for schedule changes, carrying supplies
- **Social navigation**: explaining the condition to others, managing eating situations in public[^2^]


Claim: People with diabetes make over 180 health-related decisions daily, creating significant cognitive burden
Source: ReachLink (citing Stanford University research)

Date: 2026-05-25
Excerpt: "Research estimates that people with diabetes make more than 180 health-related decisions every single day. This constant stream of calculations, adjustments, and risk assessments creates an invisible cognitive burden that most people without diabetes never see or understand."
Context: Comprehensive mental health overview citing research studies
Confidence: Medium


### 2.2 The Time Burden: ~4 Hours Daily

A nationwide survey of 674 certified diabetes educators (CDEs) estimated that the **total time needed daily for recommended diabetes self-care was approximately 4 hours for adults with type 2 diabetes and more than 5 hours for children with type 1 diabetes**[^3^]. For an adult with T2D on oral medications, routine daily self-management alone (SMBG, recordkeeping, medications, foot care, oral care, problem-solving, obtaining supplies) was estimated at **66 minutes**, while all recommended components including exercise totaled **234 minutes (~4 hours)**[^3^].

### 2.3 Perceived Mental Load: The "Invisible" Time Burden

Beyond the measurable time spent on tasks, diabetes creates a significant "mental load"—the cognitive burden of constantly thinking about the condition. A German study asked adults with T1 or T2 diabetes to estimate how many minutes per day they spend actively thinking about diabetes. The study found that this perceived daily mental load reflected an experience of time burden that is **distinct from the actual time spent on self-management activities and from diabetes-related distress**[^12^].


Claim: Perceived daily diabetes mental load (time spent thinking about diabetes) is distinct from actual self-management time and diabetes distress

Context: German online panel study of adults with T1 and T2 diabetes
Confidence: High


### 2.4 Decision Fatigue Manifestations

Decision fatigue in diabetes manifests in specific observable behaviors, particularly as patients age. Signs include: **ignoring CGM alerts more often, making fewer insulin adjustments even when needed, sticking to the same meals because they're easy, feeling overwhelmed by new technology, and avoiding thinking about diabetes unless absolutely necessary**[^13^].

## 3. The Diabetes Distress Scale (DDS): Measurement and Prevalence

### 3.1 DDS-17 Structure and Scoring

The Diabetes Distress Scale (DDS-17), developed by Polonsky et al. (2005), is the most widely used tool for measuring diabetes-specific emotional distress[^14^]. It consists of 17 items scored on a 6-point Likert scale (1 = not a problem to 6 = a very serious problem), yielding a **total distress score plus four subscale scores**:

- **Emotional Burden** (5 items): Feeling overwhelmed by diabetes, fearful about complications
- **Physician-Related Distress** (4 items): Dissatisfaction with medical care, feeling treated as a number
- **Regimen-Related Distress** (5 items): Stress about medication, diet, exercise demands
- **Interpersonal Distress** (3 items): Feeling unsupported by family/friends, social tension[^14^]

**Interpretation**: A mean item score of **<2.0** indicates little or no distress; **2.0–2.9** indicates moderate distress; and **≥3.0** indicates high distress warranting clinical concern[^14^].


Claim: DDS-17 identifies four distress domains with ≥3.0 mean score indicating high distress requiring clinical attention
Source: Diabetes Distress Scale Guide (Polonsky et al. 2005)
URL: https://resref.com/diabetes-distress-scale-guide/
Date: 2026-01-03
Excerpt: "Interpretation: <2.0: Little or no distress. 2.0–2.9: Moderate distress. ≥3.0: High distress, indicating clinical concern and potential poor glycemic control and self-care."
Context: Standardized clinical tool used worldwide
Confidence: High


### 3.2 Type 1 and Type 2 Specific Scales

More recently, type-specific distress scales have been developed. The **T1-DDS** (2015) evaluates 7 common sources of distress for T1D adults: powerlessness, negative social perceptions, physician distress, friend/family distress, hypoglycemia distress, management distress, and eating distress[^15^]. The newer **T2-DDAS** (2023) was specifically developed for insulin-using and non-insulin-using adults with T2D, reflecting contemporary diabetes issues[^15^].

## 4. CGM Alarm Fatigue: Frequency, Impact, and Management

### 4.1 The Scale of Alarm Fatigue

Alarm fatigue from continuous glucose monitors is one of the most common causes of diabetes technology discontinuation[^4^]. A narrative review found that alarm fatigue frequency varied significantly across studies, from 0% in some studies to **26–50% in others**, with diabetes technology discontinuation rates ranging from **12% to 62%**[^4^].


Claim: CGM alarm fatigue is the most common cause of diabetes technology discontinuation, with discontinuation rates of 12-62%
Source: Children (MDPI narrative review)
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12731774/
Date: 2025
Excerpt: "Alarm fatigue caused by increased use of diabetes technology is the most common cause of discontinuation of its use... frequency varied significantly from zero to 26–50%, while discontinuation percentage varied from 12 to 62%."
Context: Narrative review of alarm fatigue in children and adolescents with T1D
Confidence: High


### 4.2 Impact on Sleep and Quality of Life

The review found that alarm fatigue is **not limited to pediatric patients—it also affects parents/caregivers**[^4^]. Studies recorded alarm fatigue frequency as high as **60% for parental sleep disturbance**[^4^]. Parents reported "sleeping lightly," nocturnal awakenings, and poor sleep quality. In one study, among parents and caregivers of children with T1D using CGM, **approximately 25% admitted they remained unresponsive when CGM alarms went off repeatedly**[^4^].

A 2024 analysis found that diabetes alarm fatigue commonly led to **higher blood sugars outside of a tight target range** (70-140 mg/dL for at least 50% of the time)[^16^]. This creates a paradox: trying to stay in a tighter range generates more alarms, which leads to more distress and burnout, which worsens glycemic control[^16^].


Claim: ~25% of caregivers remained unresponsive to repeated CGM alarms; alarm fatigue led to higher blood sugars outside target range
Source: Healthline (citing 2024 analysis)
URL: https://www.healthline.com/health/diabetes/how-to-prevent-or-manage-alarm-fatigue-in-diabetes
Date: 2025-07-01
Excerpt: "In this 2024 analysis article, researchers examined four past studies and found that diabetes alarm fatigue commonly led to higher blood sugars outside of a tight target range... approximately 25% admitted that when the CGM alarms go off repeatedly, they become indifferent to the alarm."
Context: Review of research on diabetes device alarm fatigue
Confidence: High


### 4.3 The Cycle of Frustration

Alarm fatigue creates a self-reinforcing cycle: **frustration → disappointment → embarrassment → reduced adherence → discontinuation**[^4^]. Among CGM ex-users, **50% reported "too many alarms" as the cause of discontinuation**[^4^]. The phenomenon extends beyond patients to parents and caregivers, who experience their own form of alarm fatigue with associated sleep deprivation and anxiety[^4^].

### 4.4 Management Strategies

Effective strategies for managing alarm fatigue include: individualized education on alarms, psychological support, gradual adoption starting with the most important alarms, regular review of glucose data, sharing the load with family/caregivers, and using personalized alarm thresholds[^4^][^16^]. Some patients report practical hacks like making high alerts sound happier, using vibrate mode, creating nighttime profiles to minimize non-critical alerts, and periodically changing alert sounds to ensure they wake up[^17^].

## 5. Technology Burden: Data Overwhelm and App Fatigue

### 5.1 Overwhelmed by Data

A comprehensive systematic review of 54 qualitative studies (1,845 participants) on patient experiences with CGM identified a major theme: **"burdened with device complexities"**[^18^]. Patients commonly reported feeling **overwhelmed by the volume and complexity of glucose data**[^18^]. One participant described: *"It's a lot of information. It's very... it's really useful for me. And then sometimes it's not. Sometimes I have a hate relationship with my iPhone because it's really unhealthy, like it's too much information and it's kind of the same with the pump"*[^18^].

Another participant noted: *"You are under constant surveillance. And then the arrows go upwards and then they go downwards, and then the alarm sounds if it drops too fast, and if it rises too quickly you become stressed"*[^18^].


Claim: Patients feel overwhelmed by CGM data volume; unable to decipher what's useful
Source: Diabetic Medicine (Natale et al. systematic review)
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10755613/
Date: 2023
Excerpt: "CGM generated large amounts of data such that participants were unable to 'decipher what's useful and what can sort of be left out.' Participants felt demotivated by poor glucose readings."
Context: Systematic review of 54 qualitative studies, 1,845 participants
Confidence: High


### 5.2 Technology Adoption Barriers

Multiple studies using the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) and Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) have identified key barriers to diabetes app adoption. A Chinese study found that **perceived ease of use and perceived usefulness** were critical factors, with e-health literacy significantly impacting both[^19^]. Another study found that **performance expectancy and social influence** had the strongest effects on behavioral intention to use diabetes management apps, with **perceived privacy risk having a negative effect** (β=–0.073)[^20^].

Barriers identified across studies include: **low awareness, lack of perceived need, poor digital literacy, low learning motivation, vision problems, app cost, device availability, and limited phone storage**[^21^]. Despite the availability of effective apps, **only 8% of T2DM patients utilized digital support** for diabetes self-management[^21^].


Claim: Only 8% of T2DM patients utilize digital support; barriers include low awareness, poor digital literacy, cost, and privacy concerns
Source: International Journal of Diabetes and Clinical Research
URL: https://ijddc.org/doc/MBL_Fid5-10-13410_2024_1413_PrintPDF.pdf
Date: 2024
Excerpt: "Despite the availability and effectiveness of these apps, only a small percentage (8%) of the T2DM patients utilized the digital support for diabetes self-management."
Context: Study on factors influencing adoption of mobile apps for medication adherence
Confidence: Medium


### 5.3 What Patients Want from Technology

The evidence suggests patients want technology that: reduces their burden rather than adding to it, is easy to use, provides clear actionable insights rather than raw data dumps, integrates multiple functions rather than requiring multiple apps, and respects their privacy. A meta-analysis found that **technology-supported psychological interventions showed larger effects** on reducing diabetes distress[^10^], suggesting that digital tools can help when designed appropriately.

## 6. Foot Care Adherence and Reminders

### 6.1 The Prevention Gap

Foot complications represent one of the most serious and costly outcomes of diabetes. Digital interventions have shown promise in improving foot care self-management. A systematic review of 18 RCTs found that **digital intelligent interventions significantly improved self-management behaviors** in patients with diabetic foot, including diabetes control, foot care behaviors, and blood glucose monitoring[^22^].

Intervention forms included: WeChat (39%), apps (22%), electronic platforms (17%), mixed interventions (17%), and smartphone thermography (6%)[^22^]. Components included: self-management education (94%), blood glucose and foot condition monitoring (44%), self-management supervision and follow-up (33%), foot risk assessment (6%), and **foot care reminders (6%)**[^22^].


Claim: Digital intelligent interventions significantly improved self-management behaviors in patients with diabetic foot
Source: Journal of Medical Internet Research ( systematic review)
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11979535/
Date: 2025-03-25
Excerpt: "17 studies (17/18, 94%) indicated that, compared with routine care, digital intelligent interventions significantly improved the self-management behaviors of patients with DF, including diabetes control, foot care behaviors, and blood glucose monitoring."
Context: Systematic review of 18 RCTs on digital interventions for diabetic foot
Confidence: High


### 6.2 The Daily Burden of Foot Care

For elderly patients in particular, foot care represents a significant challenge. One Indian elderly participant shared: *"I'm scared to cut my own nails, thinking I might hurt myself. I rely on my grandchildren for such tasks"*[^23^]. Physical impairments including arthritis, vision problems, and mobility limitations compound the difficulty of routine foot self-examination[^23^].

## 7. Appointment Preparation Burden

### 7.1 What Patients Must Bring and Remember

The burden of preparing for diabetes appointments is substantial. Clinical guidelines recommend that patients bring: **blood glucose logs, all medications or records of medication use, blood pressure logs, healthy eating and physical activity logs, a list of concerns, and a diabetes health record card**[^24^]. Pre-visit preparation includes: reflecting on progress since the last visit, reflecting on general health and mental health, identifying ideas/concerns/expectations, and ensuring technology is configured for virtual visits[^25^].

One transition guide for adults with T1D notes that patients should: get bloodwork taken at least one week before the appointment, monitor blood sugar, track medication usage, track irregularities, communicate lifestyle changes, ask questions, and follow up on recommendations[^26^].

### 7.2 Annual Review Requirements

In the UK NHS system, the annual diabetes review includes: blood tests (HbA1c, cholesterol, kidney function), urine sample, foot check, weight/height/BMI, blood pressure, smoking status inquiry, and diabetic eye check[^27^]. The IDF recommends these checks be offered routinely, yet many patients miss them due to the cumulative burden of preparation and attendance[^27^].

## 8. Supply Management Complexity

### 8.1 The Prior Authorization Nightmare

Managing diabetes supplies represents a significant bureaucratic burden. An NPR investigation documented the case of Ric Peralta, a Dexcom user who described the prior authorization process as "maddening"[^28^]. For each component of his CGM (sensor, transmitter, receiver), he needs separate prior authorizations. Since sensors last ~10 days and his insurance allows a three-month supply, **his doctor must reconfirm medical necessity every three months**[^28^].

Peralta reported spending **four hours on the phone** with Dexcom and his insurer over six weeks to sort out a claim denial, during which time he had to revert to finger stick tests[^28^].


Claim: Prior authorization requirements force patients to spend hours on the phone; 75% of physicians say it leads patients to abandon treatment
Source: NPR
URL: https://www.npr.org/sections/health-shots/2019/09/18/744117217/its-not-just-insulin-diabetes-patients-struggle-to-get-crucial-supplies
Date: 2019-09-18
Excerpt: "Of the 1,000 physicians surveyed, 91 percent said prior authorizations 'have a negative impact on patient clinical outcomes;' 75 percent said the requirements 'can at least sometimes lead to patients abandoning a recommended course of treatment.'"
Context: NPR investigation on diabetes supply access barriers
Confidence: High


### 8.2 Financial Burden

Key insurance terms that create burden include: deductibles, copayments, coinsurance, prior authorization, quantity limits, and step therapy requirements[^29^]. The financial burden disproportionately affects elderly individuals on fixed incomes, with costs of medications, frequent checkups, and lifestyle modifications straining resources[^23^].

## 9. Family and Caregiver Support Needs

### 9.1 Caregiver Roles

A qualitative study of caregivers for veterans with diabetes identified three primary support roles: **memory support and care organizer, hands-on daily activity support, and communication/service coordinator with healthcare providers**[^30^].

Caregivers helped with: tracking and scheduling appointments, taking notes during healthcare visits, making lists of current medications, tracking blood sugar readings in logbooks, and helping prepare questions for doctors[^30^]. One caregiver shared: *"When I am in the exam room with him, I can hear the whole story and if there is a medication change or if he is supposed to do something different. I can remember a lot of things that they [doctors] are going to tell him"*[^30^].


Claim: Caregivers serve as memory support, care organizers, and communication coordinators for PwD
Source: Chronic Illness (PMC)
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10320813/
Date: 2023
Excerpt: "Caregivers of adults with diabetes assume support roles related to helping remember healthcare tasks, providing hands-on support for daily activities, and coordinating communication and services with healthcare providers."
Context: Qualitative study of caregiving for veterans with diabetes
Confidence: High


### 9.2 Communication Strategies

Family communication around diabetes is critical. Research shows that poor relationships between family members can lead to **poor diabetes self-care, high blood sugars, stress, and negative health outcomes**[^31^]. Effective strategies include: non-judgmental conversations, active listening, making behavior changes sound easier, encouragement and support, and incorporating similar goals in family lifestyle[^31^].

Importantly, family members without diabetes should be aware that their own attitudes can be extremely influential. Non-diagnosed family members' unhealthy food choices might sabotage the individual's efforts, and trying to control food preparation can lead to rebellion[^31^].

### 9.3 The Emotional Burden on Caregivers

Being a caregiver is challenging. Caregivers need their own support, and mutual understanding fosters stronger bonds. Families who invest time in learning about diabetes are not only showing support—they are actively contributing to better health outcomes[^32^].

## 10. Emergency Preparedness

### 10.1 The Complexity of Diabetes Emergency Kits

The CDC recommends that people with diabetes maintain a comprehensive emergency kit with supplies lasting **at least 1-2 weeks**[^33^]. Required items include:

- Copies of all prescriptions with current dosages
- Insulin and syringes for every injection
- All oral diabetes and prescription medicines
- Blood sugar meter with extra batteries
- Insulin pump supplies
- **Glucagon kits** (nasal or auto-injector)
- Ketone strips
- Glucose tablets or quick carbs for lows
- Cooler with cold packs for insulin storage
- Medical ID and insurance cards[^33^]

Beyond Type 1 provides additional guidance: pack double the supplies, protect insulin from water/temperature extremes, and be prepared to manage without refrigeration[^34^]. Emergency glucagon options now include Baqsimi nasal glucagon, Gvoke HypoPen auto-injector, and Zegalogue auto-injector[^34^].


Claim: Diabetes emergency preparedness requires maintaining 1-2 weeks of supplies in a comprehensive kit; lack of preparation poses serious risks
Source: CDC
URL: https://www.cdc.gov/diabetes/articles/diabetes-care-emergencies.html
Date: 2024-06-10
Excerpt: "Pack enough diabetes supplies to last at least 1 to 2 weeks, including: Insulin and syringes, oral diabetes medicines, blood sugar meter, extra batteries, insulin pump supplies, glucagon kits, ketone strips, glucose tablets."
Context: CDC diabetes emergency preparedness guidance
Confidence: High


### 10.2 The Mental Burden of Constant Vigilance

The need for emergency preparedness adds a layer of constant vigilance. Patients must track expiration dates, rotate stock, maintain backup plans for pump failure, and know how to switch to injections if needed. This represents yet another cognitive load on top of daily management.

## 11. Post-Hospital Recovery Support

### 11.1 The Transition Care Gap

Post-hospital discharge represents a critical and vulnerable period for people with diabetes. The DiaTOHC pilot study found that adding **patient-centered discharge education, HbA1c-based therapy adjustment, and post-discharge navigator support** showed promise in reducing readmission risk, particularly for patients with baseline HbA1c >7.0% who experienced a **34% relative risk reduction** in readmission/ED visits[^35^].

Post-discharge support included: phone calls 1-3 days after discharge to assess status and confirm medication receipt, weekly calls for 4 weeks, blood glucose monitoring review, and referrals for home nursing visits or community health workers for non-medical needs[^35^].


Claim: Post-discharge navigator support reduced readmission risk by 34% for patients with HbA1c >7.0%
Source: Journal of Clinical Medicine (Rubin et al., DiaTOHC study)
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8949063/
Date: 2022
Excerpt: "In this subgroup, Intervention subjects experienced a 34% relative risk reduction in readmission/ED visit risk and absolute risk reduction of 13.5%."
Context: Pilot RCT of the Diabetes Transition of Hospital Care (DiaTOHC) program
Confidence: High


### 11.2 The Patient Navigation Burden

A qualitative longitudinal study found that in the 2 months following discharge, participants had a **median of 10 encounters** with healthcare professionals (range: 6–28)[^36^]. Navigation was complex and relied heavily on patient involvement. Participants who received clear information felt more empowered, while others highlighted difficulty organizing care during this vulnerable period[^36^].

Notably, some patients refused home care services because they felt it made them seem "sick"—one participant said: *"The [homecare nurse] makes you feel like you're sick... It's a bit humiliating"*[^36^].

## 12. Meal Planning, Carb Counting, and Routine Complexity

### 12.1 The Exhaustion of Constant Food Vigilance

Meal planning and carbohydrate counting represent a fundamental and relentless source of diabetes management burden. Sources of burnout specifically include: **constant vigilance tracking blood glucose, carb intake, and insulin doses; the never-ending need to track carbs at every meal; and guilt and perfectionism when blood glucose levels are not ideal**[^37^].

The cognitive load extends beyond home cooking to social situations, dining out, and travel. One older adult study participant noted the **desire to enjoy food rather than restrict during aging years** as a significant challenge[^38^].

### 12.2 Strategies for Reduction

Evidence-based strategies for reducing meal-related burden include: **meal prepping and stocking the freezer, using carb counting apps, practicing mindful eating, simplifying meals to balanced but easy preparations, and delegating grocery shopping and meal prep to family members**[^37^].

## 13. "Diabetes Life" vs. "Normal Life": Psychosocial Impact

### 13.1 Diabetes Stigma

Diabetes stigma is defined as negative attitudes, judgment, discrimination, or prejudice against someone because they have diabetes[^5^]. The CDC reports that **more than half of people with diabetes report having experienced stigma**[^5^]. Stigma can exist anywhere: family, school, workplace, and even healthcare settings.

The IDF global survey found that **58% of PwD experienced stigma and discrimination**, and **55% reported fear of needles**[^1^]. Stigma has been associated with **lower psychosocial functioning, decreased self-care behaviors, higher HbA1c levels**, and can discourage people from performing important daily self-care including injecting insulin, checking blood sugar, or wearing diabetes devices[^5^][^39^].


Claim: >50% of people with diabetes experience stigma; associated with lower self-care, higher HbA1c, and decreased psychosocial functioning

Date: 2024-06-10

Context: CDC official guidance on diabetes stigma
Confidence: High


### 13.2 The "Diminished Self"

Qualitative research with elderly patients in India revealed the concept of the **"diminished self"**—where participants compared their current state to their past healthier selves[^23^]. One participant reflected: *"I regret not having looked after myself in the past, but now I have come to terms with it"*[^23^]. Another expressed: *"I went from being confident to somebody drained and angry"*[^23^].

Participants reported avoiding social situations: *"I avoid weddings and family gatherings due to my frailty. I also fear being questioned"*[^23^]. The stigma associated with the condition can be socially isolating—one participant shared: *"I'm tired of being called a diabetic... It's a label I will carry for the rest of my life"*[^23^].

### 13.3 Depression and Anxiety

People with diabetes are **2 to 3 times more likely to have depression** than those without the condition, and rates of anxiety are approximately **20% higher** among people managing diabetes[^2^]. Depression rates are three times higher than the general population[^2^]. This bidirectional relationship creates a vicious cycle where poor mental health worsens diabetes management, which worsens mental health.

## 14. What Patients Actually Want: Simplification, Not More Complexity

### 14.1 The Patient-Centered Care Imperative

Patient-centered care in diabetes requires: **autonomy support** (listening to what patients want), **cooperation and collaboration** (referring to non-physician professionals), **communication and education** (explaining in comprehensible ways), **emotional support** (discussing anxieties and fears), and **involvement of family** (providing information to discuss together)[^40^].

Research emphasizes that medical staff should **focus on patients' needs** in terms of listening to what they want to do before suggesting new lifestyle changes, and should **attempt to understand patients' opinions**[^40^]. Peer support in small group settings and weekly meetings can help build community[^40^].


Claim: Patient-centered diabetes care requires autonomy support, collaboration, communication, emotional support, and family involvement
Source: Patient-centered care in diabetes (PMC systematic review)
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11292325/
Date: 2024
Excerpt: "Medical staff should focus on patients' needs (autonomy support) in terms of listening what patients want to do or before suggesting a new lifestyle... medical staff should explain things in a way that patients can comprehend, respect patients' expressions."
Context: Systematic review on patient-centered care concepts in diabetes
Confidence: High


### 14.2 The Paradox of Overtreatment in Older Adults

There is a paradox where **overly intensive and complex treatment regimens are actually more common in those at highest risk of hypoglycemia**[^41^]. The ADA's 2025 Standards of Care recommends **"deintensifying"**—lowering the dose or frequency of medications that can cause hypoglycemia. Simplifying complex treatment plans can ensure individualized blood sugar goals are met without unnecessary harm[^41^].

Context: ATTD 2025 conference reporting on older adult diabetes management
Confidence: High


### 14.3 The Technology Ease-of-Use Imperative

A Technology Acceptance Model study found that for Chinese T2D patients, **perceived ease of use (β=0.380) and perceived usefulness (β=0.546)** significantly influenced attitudes toward digital disease management apps[^19^]. Electronic health literacy had a massive impact on perceived ease of use (β=0.659)[^19^]. This underscores the need for diabetes apps that are genuinely simple to use, especially for older adults with visual impairment and reduced manual dexterity[^19^].

## 15. Peer Support and Community Resources

### 15.1 Effectiveness of Peer Support

A landmark study in China demonstrated that a **community-based peer support program sustained improvements in clinical and psychosocial outcomes to 24 months**[^42^]. The collaborative model between community health centers and community organizations provided standardization along with adaptability to local needs. The balance of standardization with flexibility was key to success[^42^].

A community and peer support healthy lifestyle program (CP-HELP) in Iran found that **8 weeks of community health worker-led + peer support intervention significantly improved self-care behaviors and fasting blood glucose levels**[^43^].


Claim: Community-based peer support programs sustain improvements in clinical and psychosocial outcomes for up to 24 months

### 15.2 What Makes Peer Support Work

Peer support works through several mechanisms: **emotional closeness and shared experience**, **knowledge sharing that health workers may not have**, **continued support beyond clinical encounters**, and **helping people follow diabetes care plans**[^43^]. Peer support can enhance and complement services provided by health workers, providing continued support that bridges gaps between clinical appointments[^43^].

## 16. Travel Burden: The Logistics of Diabetes Away from Home

### 16.1 The Packing Burden

Traveling with diabetes requires meticulous preparation. The CDC recommends: **packing twice as much medicine as needed**, keeping supplies in carry-on bags, bringing a doctor's letter, carrying fast-acting sugar sources, and planning for time zone adjustments[^44^]. One travel blogger documented carrying **26 separate items** in a dedicated lunch bag for diabetes supplies alone, including multiple CGMs, backup glucometers, insulin pens, pump supplies, batteries, sugar tablets, and medications[^45^].

## 18. Forward Outlook and Emerging Trends

### 18.1 Toward the "Daily Simplicity Layer"

The evidence consistently points to a single unmet need: **diabetes management is too complex, too time-consuming, and too cognitively burdensome**. The OpenDiabetic Foundation's Lifestyle OS Strategy—positioning "DiabeticOS as the daily simplicity layer for people living with diabetes"—addresses this directly.

Key design implications from this research:

1. **Reduce decisions, don't add them**: Every feature should eliminate more decisions than it creates. The 180+ daily decision burden is the core problem.

2. **Address alarm fatigue through intelligent filtering**: CGM alarm customization and intelligent alert filtering could reduce the frustration that leads to device abandonment.

3. **Integrate, don't fragment**: Patients are overwhelmed by multiple apps, multiple notifications, and multiple data streams. Integration reduces cognitive load.

4. **Support caregivers, don't ignore them**: Family members are critical care coordinators. Tools that support caregiver communication and shared responsibility reduce patient burden.

5. **Simplify supply management**: Automated reordering, prior authorization tracking, and insurance navigation could eliminate hours of bureaucratic burden.

6. **Emergency preparedness made easy**: Pre-built emergency kits, expiration tracking, and travel checklists reduce the mental burden of constant vigilance.

7. **Peer support at scale**: Digital peer communities that provide the emotional support and shared experience that clinical care cannot offer.

8. **Patient-centered, not provider-centered**: Design for what patients want (autonomy, simplicity, understanding) rather than what providers think they need (more data, more tracking, more complexity).

### 18.2 Technology as Burden-Reducer, Not Burden-Adder

The paradox of diabetes technology is that it simultaneously reduces and adds burden. CGM reduces finger sticks but adds alarm fatigue and data overwhelm. Apps add tracking but create notification fatigue. The next generation of diabetes technology must be designed with **burden reduction as the primary success metric**, not just clinical outcomes.

As one systematic review noted: *"Education and training from health professionals may help to reduce the practical and psychological burden for better patient outcomes"*[^18^]. But education alone is not enough—the tools themselves must be designed for simplicity from the ground up.
